LEARN
Innocent people are wrongfully convicted and placed in prison year after year. Innocent people have served years in prison for a crime they did not commit. Being jailed because of biases, false conscience, negligence or legal system mistakes is unacceptable and wrong. Thousands of people have been ripped away from their families and have had years of their lives taken away from them.

Life in prison for many people is a difficult one. Prisons around the country have many flaws and insecurities that make the years spent in them feel cruel and daunting. Many prisons are unsanitary and even today some are refusing to have any covid protection and are refusing the vaccine. Violent fights break out which endangers the lives of all inmates, especially those who are mistakenly and wrongly placed there. Lastly, an issue that usually occurs in private prisons, is the lack of oversight and correction officer involvement in outbursts from inmates. (scroll for more)

As life in prison can be difficult, life after prison can be a struggle also, even for an exoneree. The psychological and physical impact of prison is an issue to not be ignored. Secondly, as a former inmate is adjusting to new life after being in prison for many years, they will come across many issues with job applications, finances, and housing. In terms of the psychological impact, many inmates feel alienated and become socially withdrawn. Additionally, they may develop an interpersonal distrust, hypervigilance and post-traumatic stress reactions. Lasty, they can form a diminished sense of self-worth and personal value.

97% of exonerees were wrongfully convicted of committing sexual assault and/or murder. Although these individuals were innocent of these crimes, approximately 25% had confessed and 11% had pleaded guilty.


In the US, there have been 1467 exonerations, 321 of them involving DNA.


27 states provide compensation for each year the exonerees were wrongfully imprisoned once they’ve been released.


Women are disproportionately incarcerated in jails where more than half of them have not yet been convicted of a crime and are still presumed innocent.


Most female exonerees were convicted of crimes that never occurred.

“DNA exoneration” is the process of a person being proved innocent post-conviction through DNA testing and evidence.


75% of DNA exonerations stem from misidentification from an eyewitness.


In ¼ of all the wrongful conviction cases, incriminating or false statements/confessions were the cause of imprisonment. In roughly 1/10 cases, the individuals had actually plead guilty to charges.


63% of false confessors were under the age of 25, and 32% were under 18; yet of all persons arrested for murder and rape, only 8 and 16%, respectively, are juveniles.


35% of the 317 cases exonerated by DNA receive no compensation.
